Loading... video streaming wars in Singapore

Just a few days ago, Singaporean cable TV providers announced the removal of Disney channels from their bundles, probably invoking much dismay (especially when TV plays an important part in keeping children entertained with disproportionate stay-home time). This move, however, heralds a new age for the Disney media empire, with its upcoming launch of Disney+.

Disney has already made an initial foray (ignoring VPNs) into the OTT video space in Singapore: it made Hotstar available to migrant workers in early May, allowing free access to a wealth of South Asian content1. Following a similar preparation window as observed in the UK 2, we can possibly expect to see Disney+ enter the Singapore market in 3-4 months.

Getting the price right is highly challenging - especially when most adult consumers today have been raised on a diet of free content on YouTube and (unfortunately) pirate sites. Resistance towards paying is fairly common in the Singapore market: a pre-Netflix era study by IMDA showed >75% of respondents would not consider paying for online video in the future [^3]. In a more recent Brightcove study [^4], almost 50% are not willing to pay (with or without ads); only 10% are open to a pure SVOD model. Will Disney+'s single-tier pricing strategy work in Singapore? Lower price point vs. Netflix might provide stronger push for customers to try the platform; but, Netflix has actually increased its prices earlier this year [^5], demonstrating high level of confidence in the attractiveness of its content offer and user stickiness (or inertia). Disney might be able to achieve the same or even better given the strength of its content portfolio - initial responses to Disney’s content in the States showed a greater focus on its library content [^6]. Nostalgia value perhaps, especially for adults who watched Disney’s classic animated features like Lion King or Little Mermaid while growing up. (I did, and I am all prepared to relive the animated Mulan experience before watching the live-action version.)

But rather than a race to the bottom, it might be interesting if something similar to Hulu’s hybrid SVOD/AVOD tiers is introduced after a “soft” launch in the market. In particular, some of Hulu’s more innovative ad formats might make the AVOD experience much more palatable to Singaporeans. For instance - giving viewers a choice over what types of ads they will like to see, or the option to binge watch ads at the beginning vs. standard commercial breaks [^7]. These might prove highly appealling to Singaporean consumers with generally lower ad exposure tolerance than their regional peers [^4].

Who’s up for Disney+?

Till then.
